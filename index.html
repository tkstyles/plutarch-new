Parallel Lives by Plutarch

Plutarch was a Greek historian, biographer, and moral philosopher born in Chaeronea, a small town in Boeotia, Greece. Educated in Athens, he later traveled widely throughout the Roman Empire and became a Roman citizen, taking the name Lucius Mestrius Plutarchus. Though deeply rooted in Greek culture, he admired many aspects of Roman life and worked to bridge the two civilizations. Plutarch is best known for his biographical series Parallel Lives, a collection of paired biographies of prominent Greek and Roman figures. Each pair compares a Greek and a Roman—such as Alexander the Great and Julius Caesar—highlighting their virtues, vices, leadership qualities, and moral character. His goal was not just to record historical events, but to explore how character shapes destiny. The work is both historical and philosophical, offering insights into leadership, ethics, and human nature. Parallel Lives had a profound influence on Renaissance thinkers and writers like Shakespeare and Montaigne, and it remains a foundational text for the study of classical antiquity and moral biography.
