Sertorius

Plutarch's biography of Quintus Sertorius is a remarkable and poignant chronicle of a Roman general who, in exile, came to embody the very ideals of the Republic he was forced to fight against. Sertorius was a man of immense talent, a brilliant military strategist, and a natural leader who commanded the unwavering loyalty of his men. Plutarch portrays him as a figure of deep honor and virtue, a stark contrast to the corrupt and ambitious generals of his time. His life was a testament to the idea that a man could create a new kind of Roman society in the most unlikely of places. The Guerrilla Master and the White Fawn Sertorius's story begins with his opposition to Sulla's tyranny, a decision that forced him to flee Rome. He found his destiny in Hispania (modern-day Spain), where he created a powerful and independent state. Plutarch details his genius for guerrilla warfare, a skill that earned him the fear and respect of Rome. He was a master of unconventional tactics, using his knowledge of the rugged terrain to outmaneuver the larger, more disciplined Roman legions sent against him. To maintain control and inspire his men, he used a unique form of psychological warfare. Plutarch recounts the legend of the white fawn, a deer he claimed was a gift from the goddess Diana. Sertorius would often consult the fawn before making a decision, a clever ruse that solidified his image as a divinely guided leader and instilled a deep sense of trust in his local allies. The Architect of a New Rome Sertorius was more than just a guerrilla leader; he was a visionary. Plutarch describes his efforts to create a "New Rome" in Hispania. He established a Senate composed of Roman exiles and local chieftains, a government that reflected his belief in justice and fairness. He founded a school for the children of local nobles, where they were taught Latin and Greek and trained in the Roman military arts. This was a testament to his ambition to create a lasting and powerful state that blended the best of Roman civilization with the local culture. His efforts were so successful that he was able to hold off the combined might of Rome for years, frustrating even the brilliant general Pompey the Great, who was sent to defeat him. The Betrayal and the Tragic End Plutarch's biography of Sertorius is, in the end, a tragic tale of betrayal. Despite his military victories and the unwavering loyalty of his men, he was ultimately undone by the political intrigues and jealousies within his own ranks. His trusted lieutenant, Perperna, grew envious of his power and conspired with others to assassinate him at a banquet. The death of Sertorius led to the swift collapse of his Spanish state, as his successors lacked his genius and his moral authority. Plutarch concludes with the sad observation that a man who had resisted the entire might of Rome was brought down by the envy of a few.
