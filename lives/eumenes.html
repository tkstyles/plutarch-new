Eumenes

Plutarch's biography of Eumenes of Cardia is a poignant and often tragic tale of a man who, though not a Macedonian, became one of the most capable and loyal of Alexander the Great's successors. Eumenes was a Greekling, a man from a small town in the Chersonese, who rose from the humble position of Alexander's chief secretary to become a brilliant general. Plutarch emphasizes his intellectual and administrative talents, cultivated from a young age, which earned him a place in the retinue of both Philip II and Alexander. His quick wit, self-control, and prudence were his most potent weapons, qualities that set him apart from the more boisterous and ambitious Macedonian generals.

After the sudden death of Alexander, the empire fractured into a chaotic struggle for power among the Diadochi. Eumenes, however, stood apart from the self-serving ambitions of his rivals. Plutarch portrays him as the one man who remained steadfastly loyal to the memory and family of Alexander. He fought not for his own gain, but to preserve the unity of the empire and the rights of Alexander's rightful heirs. This loyalty, however, was a double-edged sword. It made him a pariah to the other generals, who saw his fidelity as a direct threat to their own schemes. Eumenes was an outsider in a world of powerful Macedonians, and he had to constantly prove himself in battle and through clever political maneuvering.

Plutarch recounts Eumenes's military career as a series of brilliant campaigns against a coalition of enemies determined to destroy him. He was a master of improvisation, able to raise armies from a diverse population and inspire a fierce loyalty among his troops. His most famous victory was against Craterus, one of Alexander's most beloved and respected generals. Eumenes won the battle but was plagued by the knowledge that he had killed a fellow Macedonian, an act that intensified the hatred against him. His life was a constant flight from enemies, a testament to his tactical brilliance and his remarkable ability to escape from seemingly impossible situations. He was a master of deception and a general who knew how to turn his weaknesses into strengths.

Plutarch's account culminates in a final, devastating betrayal.
