Sulla

Plutarch's biography of Lucius Cornelius Sulla is a compelling and unsettling study of one of Rome's most complex figures. A patrician by birth, Sulla was a man of contrasts: a brilliant general who despised democracy, a patron of the arts who unleashed a reign of terror, and a dictator who willingly surrendered his power. Plutarch paints him as a man of two faces, one of a refined intellectual and the other of a ruthless, vengeful warrior. His life was a whirlwind of political intrigue, military campaigns, and bloody civil war that would forever change the Roman Republic.

Sulla first rose to prominence under the command of Gaius Marius, his future nemesis. Plutarch details Sulla’s strategic brilliance in the Jugurthine War and, most notably, his audacious capture of King Jugurtha, a feat that brought him immense fame and, more importantly, fueled the bitter rivalry between him and Marius. As Sulla's reputation grew, so did the animosity between the two men, culminating in a vicious civil war that saw control of Rome swing back and forth between their factions. Sulla's military genius was further demonstrated in his campaigns in the East, where he defeated King Mithridates VI of Pontus, a formidable enemy who had massacred tens of thousands of Roman citizens.

After a series of brutal battles, Sulla finally returned to Rome and seized absolute power. This is where Plutarch's account becomes most chilling. In order to consolidate his rule and settle old scores, Sulla instituted the infamous proscriptions, a list of his political opponents who were to be executed without trial. Plutarch describes how these lists grew, often including men of wealth and influence who were killed simply for their property. The sheer brutality and scale of the proscriptions, which saw thousands of Romans murdered, left an indelible stain on his legacy and set a dangerous precedent for future civil conflicts. Yet, Sulla was not a man who sought to rule for life. After reforming the Roman constitution to strengthen the Senate and limit the power of the popular assemblies—reforms designed to restore the old Republican order—he did the unthinkable: he voluntarily abdicated his dictatorship and retired to his country estate.

Plutarch's biography of Sulla is a profound and disturbing examination of a man who used tyranny to try and save a republic that was already in its death throes. He was a man who believed in the old Roman virtues but was willing to commit the most horrific acts to preserve them. His life is a cautionary tale about the corrosive nature of power and a tragic testament to the idea that sometimes, in the fight for a cause, a man can destroy the very thing he seeks to save. He left behind a "peace" that was built on a foundation of terror, and his example would serve as a grim inspiration for future Roman strongmen.
