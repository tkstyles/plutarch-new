Lysander

Plutarch's Life of Lysander is the portrait of a man who was the very antithesis of the traditional Spartan ideal. While other Spartans were known for their rugged simplicity, unwavering discipline, and disdain for wealth, Lysander was a man of ambition, cunning, and political dexterity. He was a master of diplomacy and deception, a formidable figure who, more than any other individual, was responsible for Sparta's ultimate victory in the Peloponnesian War against Athens.

The Architect of a Naval Empire Lysander's most significant achievement, and the one that cemented his place in history, was his mastery of naval warfare. This was a skill not typically associated with the land-based Spartans. Plutarch details how Lysander, through a combination of charisma and political savvy, cultivated a powerful friendship with Cyrus the Younger, the Persian prince. This alliance proved to be a game-changer, providing Lysander with the financial backing to build and maintain a massive fleet, an asset Athens could not match. His brilliant and patient strategy culminated in the decisive naval Battle of Aegospotami, where he caught the Athenian fleet unprepared and utterly destroyed it, effectively ending the war and bringing Athens to its knees.

The Conqueror and the Corrupter With the victory in his grasp, Lysander became the de facto ruler of the Greek world. He installed pro-Spartan oligarchies, known as the "Decarchies," in the cities he conquered, cementing his personal power and the Spartan hegemony. But this was a departure from the traditional Spartan way of life. Plutarch observes that Lysander, while outwardly embodying Spartan discipline, introduced foreign wealth and a corrupting influence into Sparta itself. He allowed his friends to amass fortunes, and his personal power became a source of envy and suspicion within the Spartan elite. The Rise and Fall of a Kingmaker As Lysander's influence grew, so did the opposition against him. His attempts to change the Spartan constitution to allow for an elective monarchy, which would have made him king, were thwarted. Plutarch shows how Lysander, once the hero of Sparta, became a figure of controversy and suspicion. His final campaign, in which he was killed in a battle at Haliartus, was a reflection of his later life: a messy and ill-conceived affair that ended in his inglorious death. Plutarch's biography of Lysander is a cautionary tale about the complexities of power and ambition. He was the man who won a war for his city but, in doing so, introduced the very elements that would lead to its eventual decline. His life serves as a fascinating study of a brilliant strategist who was, in the end, perhaps too clever for his own good.
